Course Description

This course introduces theory and practice of structuring information and designing visual representations for effective communication.

Learning Outcomes

  • Explore the role and influence that graphic design, information architecture and user experience play on our perception and interpretation of information.
  • Explain key methods used in the context of information design to visually represent different forms of information.
  • Generate design criteria from specific scenarios and assess the utility of the criteria in the development of a user-centred design.

Learning Outcomes (continued)

  • Use methods - e.g. sketching, wireframing, sitemaps and flowcharts - to design applications that will translate basic qualitative and quantitative information into more human-readable representations.
  • Demonstrate key principles of graphic design, information architecture and user experience design in the creation of websites (using HTML/CSS).

Participation will be checked at random points through the term. Participation can only be excused for legitimate reasons (i.e. doctor's notes, large calamities, emergencies). When in doubt, email (mlockyer@sfu.ca).

Readings

All readings are provided on Canvas digitally through the SFU Library or as PDFs.

Quizzes

There will be 3 quizzes included in the course this term. Two will be reading-based quizzes, and the final will focus on HTML/CSS knowledge.

Assignments

Assignments are always completed individually and are your first opportunity to work on concepts for your projects.

Projects

There are three projects in the course this term, which build upon one another; the first one defines the context and scope of your project, the second concludes with wireframing, and the third with a complete HTML/CSS mockup of a website. Projects are completed in groups of three, unless special permission is given by Andrew. All project process materials are graded individually.

Teamwork

You will be allowed to select your groups for any teamwork in this course. Process deliverables for each project are graded individually, so each team member is responsible for completing their own work. Consider your group member options carefully.

All Courses Are Equal

Please remember that all courses are created equal, no one course should monopolize all your time at the detriment of your other courses. If you should find yourself in such a situation, please let your instructor know sooner rather than later.

Late or Incorrectly Submitted Deliverables

Late deliverables receive 10% per day late starting immediately after the deliverable is due. Allow yourself sufficient time to submit deliverables without incurring late penalties.

Files incorrectly submitted to Canvas

For example, submitting a PDF that cannot be opened - will receive a penalty on the resubmitted file of 25% plus 10% per day not resubmitted (after the grades have been released). For example, if you do not resubmit the file until two days after the grades are released the penalty will be 45%. It is your responsibility to ensure that your deliverables make it in on-time and in an uncorrupted fashion.

Lab Switching

Is not permitted unless under special circumstances. You must first speak with Andrew (mlockyer@sfu.ca), though keep in mind that there must be an opening and nobody on the waitlist to be able to switch labs (often unlikely).

Plagiarism

If your work refers to or builds upon an existing work, you must cite the original in your submission or presentation. Failure to do so will be considered plagiarism. Plagiarism will result in a grade reduction or school disciplinary action at the instructor's discretion.
